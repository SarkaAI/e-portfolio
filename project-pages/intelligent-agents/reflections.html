Reflections
Personal reflections on what I learned and how my thinking evolved during the Intelligent Agents module.
Overview
This module changed how I think about autonomy in agentic AI, and in any application that acts on its own. At the start, I understood an intelligent agent mainly as a definition: something that perceives its environment and acts to achieve goals. By the end, having built a multi-agent system myself, I understand autonomy as a responsibility to be bounded, verified, and explained. The most important lessons did not come from things working; they came from claims that turned out to be wrong, and from failures of my own system that each taught me something a working run never would.
From Design to Implementation
The turning point of the module was the tutor feedback on our group design proposal for a multi-agent research assistant, the system I would later name MASRA (A Multi-Agent System Research Assistant) during my individual build. We had proposed it with confidence, and the feedback acknowledged the strengths, but it also exposed a contradiction we had not seen: we claimed a blackboard-style shared memory while choosing CrewAI, an orchestration framework whose native model is a pipeline. We had also attributed model loading behaviour to the orchestrator that actually belongs to the model engine. Reading that feedback was uncomfortable, because these were not typos; they were claims we had made without verifying how the tools actually work.
When I implemented the design individually, I decided the honest response was not to defend the proposal but to correct it. I moved from the proposal's original CrewAI framework to LangGraph, where the shared state object genuinely is the blackboard we had claimed, removed the fictional lazy-loading logic, and recorded every deviation from the proposal in a dedicated document with its reason. This changed my understanding of what design documents are for: they are commitments that implementation will test, not marketing. The gap between what a framework is claimed to do and what it actually does became, for me, the central technical risk of agent-based development.
Building Trust in Autonomous Systems
The deepest technical learning came from the trust subsystem. The feedback had challenged our loop limits: what happens if the planner produces a bad plan the system cannot fix? My answer was to bound the autonomy three ways: a capped replan counter, a hard recursion limit, and per-call token and timeout limits. When the cap is reached, the run still completes, but the report is clearly flagged as low confidence. Watching a real run terminate at the cap and honestly label its own output as provisional, instead of presenting it as verified, was the moment the concept of safe, bounded autonomy became concrete for me.
I made a similar decision about explainability. It was tempting to ask the model to explain its own reasoning, but a model explaining itself risks post-hoc rationalisation: the explanation is generated by the same process it claims to describe. Instead, the explainability layer is plain deterministic code that restates recorded provenance: which sources grounded each section, what verification concluded, which gaps were counted, and how the confidence score was computed. Testing also kept correcting my assumptions: credibility scoring initially buried foundational papers that exist only as preprints, and a heavily cited but off-topic paper once ranked top for a question it had nothing to do with. Each of these failures forced a design decision and taught me that trust in agent output has to be engineered, not assumed. The design lesson I take forward is that trustworthiness came less from adding intelligence and more from removing it where it was not needed: deterministic checks, arithmetic confidence, provenance explanations, and a human reading real output critically.
Communication, Security, and Broader Implications
The collaborative discussions widened my perspective beyond my own system. Exploring agent communication languages such as KQML showed me that communication between autonomous agents is not just a technical convenience but a governance question: every performative an agent accepts is something another party can exploit. Connecting this to modern LLM agents, especially indirect prompt injection through trusted content, linked the module's classical material directly to my professional interest in cybersecurity.
The discussion on deep learning ethics reinforced this. The same generative capability that supports defenders supports attackers, and the dual-use tension cannot be engineered away; it has to be governed. Frameworks such as the EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework gave me a vocabulary for that governance. Together with my implementation experience, these discussions convinced me that responsible deployment is a property of the whole socio-technical system, not of the model.
Collaboration and Contribution
Working in Group D on the design proposal required coordinating architecture decisions, requirements analysis, and writing across time zones in a fully virtual environment. I contributed to the system design and pushed mainly for the verification, gap analysis, and confidence mechanisms; the explainability approach came from a teammate, and the trust subsystem the feedback praised for engineering foresight was genuinely a joint result. Working together and building something shaped by each other's experience and points of view, and then carrying the design forward on my own with all the lessons learned, taught me that professional collaboration includes how you respond to feedback on shared work: without defensiveness, and with the discipline to document what changed and why.
The peer discussions were also collaboration in a real sense. Responding to peers on communication overheads and on security incidents pushed me to research beyond the module materials, from KQML facilitator agents to enterprise controls for modern agent protocols, and my own posts improved because others challenged them.
Professional Development and Action Plan
This module strengthened skills I will use directly in practice: architecting multi-agent systems, evaluating orchestration frameworks against their actual semantics, writing testable safety bounds, and documenting design decisions honestly. It also strengthened a habit of mind: distrusting unverified claims, including my own.
Going forward, I want to deepen three areas. First, formal evaluation of agent systems, moving beyond counting tests towards benchmarks for groundedness and calibration. Second, the security of agent-to-agent communication, where my cybersecurity background and this module's material meet. Third, scaling from a single bounded workflow to systems where multiple autonomous agents interact, where emergent behaviour makes the safety questions harder and more interesting.
Conclusion
The module took me through a complete professional cycle: theory, collaborative design, critical feedback, individual implementation, testing, and honest evaluation. What I take away most is a standard: an autonomous system I build should be able to show where its answers came from, admit what it could not verify, and stop when its bounds are reached. That standard now feels less like a module requirement and more like my own.
