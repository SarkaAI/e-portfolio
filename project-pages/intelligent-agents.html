Intelligent Agents
Exploring agent-based computing : the theory, tools, and techniques behind autonomous systems that perceive their environment and act to achieve their goals.
Learning Outcomes
Across this module, I moved from understanding intelligent agents as a theoretical concept to designing, building, and critically evaluating a working multi-agent system of my own.
In the early units, I built a foundation in agent architectures and their trade-offs: reactive and deliberative approaches, the Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) model, and hybrid designs that combine them. I learned to see architecture choice as a decision about risk and uncertainty rather than a purely technical preference. Later units on agent communication languages such as KQML, ontologies, and speech acts showed me how independent agents can coordinate meaningfully, and also how communication becomes a security surface, for example through indirect prompt injection in modern LLM-based agents.
The centre of the module was applying this knowledge to a real system. In the group phase, our team designed a multi-agent research assistant for academic literature, using the Tropos methodology to map requirements onto agent goals and a BDI-inspired planning loop. In the individual phase, I implemented the design, which I named MASRA (A Multi-Agent System Research Assistant) during the build, as an eight-role, nine-node system on LangGraph with fully local models via Ollama: a planner, retrieval from three academic APIs, grounded summarisation over a ChromaDB vector store, synthesis, claim-by-claim verification, gap analysis, confidence scoring, and a deterministic explainability layer.
A defining part of my learning came from tutor feedback on the group design, which identified a contradiction between our claimed blackboard memory and the framework we chose, and a factual error about model loading. Rather than repeating these claims, I corrected them in the implementation and recorded every deviation with its reason. This taught me that critically evaluating my own design is as important a skill as producing it.
Finally, the module shaped my professional perspective on autonomous systems: agents that act on their own need bounded autonomy, verifiable outputs, and explanations grounded in traceable evidence rather than model self-reporting. My biggest learning was a design lesson I will take forward: trustworthiness came less from adding intelligence and more from removing it where it was not needed, through deterministic checks, arithmetic confidence, provenance explanations, and a human reading real output critically. Working in a virtual team, coordinating roles, and responding constructively to critical feedback strengthened the collaborative skills that agent development demands in practice.
